Example 2.11. (an invariant trigonometric polynomial) 

A multivariate polynomial can be expressed uniquely in the Chebyshev basis. 

> Type,n := 'G',2; #type, dimension
f := TPoly(Type,[2,0]) + 2*TPoly(Type,[0,1]); #objective function in polynomial Chebyshev basis
 

 

Type,n≔G,2
f≔6⁢z12−2⁢z1−1 (3.1)
 

 

When the variables z_i are replaced by the generalized cosines, then we obtain a trigonometric polynomial, which is invariant under the multiplicative Weyl group action. 

> GenCos:=GeneralizedCosine(Type,n,[u[1],u[2],-u[1]-u[2]]); #generalized cosines
F:=expand(subs([seq(z[i]=GenCos[i],i=1..n)],f)); #objective function as an invariant trigonometric polynomial
 

 

GenCos≔cos⁡2⁢π⁢u1−u23+cos⁡2⁢π⁢2⁢u1+u23+cos⁡2⁢π⁢2⁢u2+u13&comma;cos⁡6⁢π⁢u23+cos⁡2⁢π⁢3⁢u1+3⁢u23+cos⁡6⁢u1⁢π3

Similarly, when the variables z_i are replaced by the orbit polynomial, then we obtain a Laurent polynomial, which is invariant under the Weyl group. 

> theta:=FundamentalInvariant(Type,n); #fundamental invariant Laurent polynomials
inv:=expand(subs([seq(z[i]=theta[i],i=1..n)],f)); #objective function as an invariant Laurent polynomial
 

 

θ≔x16+x26⁢x1+x126⁢x2+x26⁢x12+x16⁢x2+16⁢x1&comma;x26+x226⁢x13+x136⁢x2+x136⁢x22+x26⁢x13+16⁢x2
inv≔x223⁢x13+x133⁢x2+x133⁢x22+x23⁢x13+13⁢x2+x23+x226⁢x12+x146⁢x22+x226⁢x14+x126⁢x22+x126+16⁢x12 (3.3)
 

 

The Laurent polynomial can again be written as a unique polynomial in the fundamental invariants: 

> InvariantRewrite(Type,n,inv); #write an invariant Laurent polynomial in polynomial standard basis
 

6⁢z12−2⁢z1−1 (3.4)
 

 

Similarly, it can be written in the Chebyshev basis: 

> ChebyshevInvariantRewrite(Type,n,inv); #write an invariant Laurent polynomial in polynomial Chebyshev basis
 

2⁢T0,1+T2,0 (3.5)
 

 

We plot the trigonometric polynomial: 

> plot3d([F],u[1]=-1..1,u[2]=-1..1,grid=[200,200],
orientation=[130,-60,180],
labelfont=["TimesNewRoman", 15],
labels=[u[1],u[2],'f(u)'],
size=[1000,1000]); #plot of the trigonometric polynomial
